This week, I spoke with Kevin Bachelor, a 3rd Year Computer Science Major and Minor in Theatre about his involvement with CMPM 115 Lead by Design: Experiential Learning Collaborative. Kevin reflected on what he will take away from the  experience overall, the commitment it takes to land this, why it’s worth it, and how he is applying what he’s gained from Lead by Design to other aspects of his life.  He credits the lead instructor (Tela Favoloro) for her steady support throughout the project design period,  but cautions applicants to  prepare for the complexity and workload ahead.


Q: What is the title of the class you are going to teach?

A:  Introduction to Machine Learning (CMPM 17-02)

Q: What initially motivated you to join the Lead By Design course? What about the program attracted you?
A:  I wanted to make machine learning concepts more accessible. Right now, there are math-heavy prerequisites to courses offered by the school that make it harder for students to get started, so this was a chance to help create a course that simplifies that process and opens doors for more people.
Q: Were your expectations for the course different from how the experience actually turned out?
A:  I went in with very few expectations—I knew we’d end up with a course we’d teach, but I didn’t realize just how teaching-oriented the program would be. It felt more like an education course, which I wasn’t expecting but really appreciated. I already teach through SCAI workshops, but this program formalized those skills and gave me a better understanding of behind-the-scenes techniques for effective teaching.
Q: What advice would you give to new students to set realistic expectations?
A:  Stay ahead—it’s easy to fall behind, and catching up can be overwhelming. Also, work with people you enjoy being around. The collaboration makes the workload more manageable, especially when things get tough.
Q: Was there enough guidance or resources available to you?
A:  Yes, the support was great, especially from Tela, who gave constant feedback and guidance. However, the workload was intense and I found some of the required aspects of the course cumbersome,  so balancing it all could still be a challenge.
Q: Was there any aspect of the course’s structure, content, or teaching responsibilities that felt particularly overwhelming or unclear?
A:  The workload often felt closer to 20–30 hours a week, which is more than expected for a 5-unit course. The use of SCRUM as a workflow added complexity that didn’t always feel necessary.
Q: Would having more tailored timelines or preliminary preparation, like a boot camp, help?
A:  While we did some prep work over the summer, the framework was already in place, so a boot camp isn’t necessary. Clearer expectations about the time commitment would be more helpful.
Q: What will you remember most about this experience 10 years from now?
A:  Ten years from now, I’ll probably remember the education skills the most. While I might not recall the specifics of learning them, I’ll definitely remember how I’ve implemented those skills into almost everything I do.
Q: What would you say to someone who is considering joining Lead By Design?
A:  Be prepared for the time commitment—it’s a lot. But if you’re passionate about teaching and want to make an impact, it’s incredibly rewarding. Work with people you enjoy, stay on top of the workload, and don’t hesitate to ask for help when you need it.

Have you ever wondered what it’s like to teach your own class? Now’s your chance to become a student professor!

Want to Join CMPM 115: Lead by Design?, keep your potential topic in mind! Through this 5-unit course, upper division students with a range of technical skill sets they are passionate about sharing will be guided through the process of developing a 3 unit engineering project-based design lab. You will then be paid to teach the course to lower division students in a subsequent quarter. Lead by Design requires a team-based application, and is tailored to upper division students.

Applications are now open for Spring Quarter! To have priority, submit by March 8. After this date, positions are approved on a rolling basis.
