This week, I spoke with Olivia Hargrove, a first-year Electrical Engineering major, about her first 3D printing project at Slugworks. She created a custom light-up picture frame to honor the memory of her cat who recently passed away.

Q: What inspired this project?

A: I wanted to make something to honor him, so I designed a picture frame that will light up once I add the LED panel. I had never really 3D printed anything before, so this was a learning experience. It was really exciting to make.

Q: How did you learn about Slugworks?
A: I saw stuff about it at Cornucopia and also through the Rocket Team, which I joined recently. When I found out I could use the 3D printers, I thought, "Oh, shoot, let me try something."

Q: What materials did you use?
A: Everything is 3D printed with PLA, including the light guide insert. It’s made of three different parts– the frame, the insert, and the LED panel that’s coming later. The light guide helps spread the light evenly so it won’t just be little dots.

Q: Did you run into any challenges? 
A: The biggest challenge was making sure the LED panel I ordered would fit in the frame. The instructions weren’t very clear about which one to get, so I had to take a guess. Other than that, learning how to use the 3D printers here was actually really simple.

Q: Have you worked on anything else here?

A: For Valentine's Day, I made little 3D-printed slugs for my roommates and put their names on them. It was my first time adding text to a design, so I learned a lot about how to use the software. In the future, I want to make a surfboard rack for my bike. I’ve seen them for motorcycles, and I think it’d be cool to design something similar for a regular bike.

I’m just really grateful to be in this space. There’s so much to explore, and I want to keep learning what’s possible here.

Have you ever wondered what it’s like to teach your own class? Now’s your chance to become a student group tutor!

Join CMPM 115: Lead by Design, keep your potential topic in mind! Through this 5-unit course, upper division students with a range of technical skill sets they are passionate about sharing will be guided through the process of developing a 3 unit engineering project-based design lab. You will then be paid to teach the course to lower division students in a subsequent quarter. Lead by Design requires a team-based application, and is tailored to upper division students.
