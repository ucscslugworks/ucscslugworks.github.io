This week, I talked with Naomi Prince, a Second Year Anthropology and Environmental Studies major, and Chloe Barnard, a Fourth Year Business Management Economics major, about their work on fog net research with Dr. Peter Weiss. Their project explores how fog collection can be a sustainable water source, especially in drought-prone areas. They have been using Slugworks to make parts for the fog nets and have learned how to use tools like laser cutters and drills.

Q: Why did you first come to Slugworks?
A: We found out about Slugworks through our boss, Dr. Peter Weiss. He suggested we work on our research project on fog collection here. We needed to drill materials for our fog nets but didn’t have the right equipment. We got certified and started using the drills, and then we realized there was so much more here. The laser cutter was especially cool. We used it to make QR-coded signs for each fog collector. Nick has been super helpful with navigating the shop and has made some awesome suggestions.

Q: Where is your research based?
A: We work at the old trailer park, now called the Commuter Lounge. It used to be student housing, but the trailers were recently removed. We have a storage pod there with materials and build the fog collectors outside in the dirt. It’s a very hands-on project.

Q: What tools have you used the most?
A: It’s been a mix. Our engineering interns helped a lot with the drilling. We had to drill holes through metal brackets to attach bamboo poles and plastic pipes. We could drill through plastic and bamboo ourselves, but not metal, so Slugworks was a huge help for that. The laser cutter was another big one. We used it to make QR-coded signs for each fog collector.

Q: What is the goal of building these fog nets?
A: Dr. Weiss has researched fog collection for over 20 years. He got a climate conservation grant to study how sustainable fog nets are for water collection. Fog is a water source that usually can’t be collected unless you use special mesh nets. In California, summers are foggy but dry, so this project looks at fog as an alternative water source when there is no rain. Right now, we are testing this through a fog collector adoption program in Pacifica, where we are distributing 12 fog collectors to homes, schools, and community gardens. Participants will check in monthly to report how well they work and how much supplemental water they still need.

Q: How do the fog nets handle wind?
A: They are designed to be flexible. The mesh is attached to the bamboo with Velcro straps. If all four sides are strapped down, strong winds can make the fog catcher fall over. We ask participants to unfasten two sides of the Velcro when it gets windy so it flaps around like a flag instead. Even in strong winds, the structure is stable since bamboo poles are durable.

Q: What is your research team like?
A: There are five of us: Naomi, myself, and three engineering interns, Nina, Cameron, and Aarushi. Dr. Weiss works closely with us and collaborates with many researchers and scientists.

Q: Were there any challenges using machines at Slugworks?
A: Definitely. Neither of us had used laser cutters before, so there was a learning curve. The biggest thing was making sure we used everything safely. The Canvas certification courses were helpful, and the Slugworks staff walked us through everything. Another challenge was getting things to line up during drilling. Each bamboo pole is slightly different, so even small measurement errors meant re-drilling.

Q: When did this project start and where is it now?
A: Dr. Weiss has been studying fog collection for years, but this initiative started in October when we were hired to work on marketing, social media, and logistics. Before that, there wasn’t much online presence. We helped set up an Instagram (@FogNet_ucsc) and updated the website (fognet.sites.ucsc.edu).

Right now, we have 12 fog collectors ready to go. Our first installation is on President’s Day weekend, setting up four collectors and making sure adopters know how to maintain them.

Q: Are there future plans for expansion?
A: Yes. Dr. Weiss got an extension on our current grant through March and a new grant to test fog nets in vineyards. The goal is to see if fog collection can help grapevines grow in dry areas. If it works, it could become a commercial product.

We are also working with the Seymour Marine Discovery Center on a fog-themed exhibit. It will have a mini fog collector and possibly a fog mister that visitors can activate. We are testing different sensor technologies to track water collection more accurately.

Q: What has been your favorite part of the project?
A: Working outside and using natural materials has been great. It’s so different from sitting at a computer all day. Also, meeting the team. Our interns joined because they are passionate about environmental engineering, and it’s inspiring to work with people who care about sustainability.

Telling people about fog collection has also been fun. Most have never heard of it, so it’s cool to introduce them to something new. I want to go into climate-focused entrepreneurship, so this has been a great experience in environmental conservation and business planning.

Q: What skills have you gained at Slugworks?
A: Definitely laser cutting and drilling. We also learned how to use Adobe Illustrator since we had to convert our designs into vector files for the laser cutter. Just learning about all the tools here has been really interesting.

Q: Would you like to see anything new at Slugworks?
A: A jewelry-making area would be awesome. We heard there might be plans to turn the spray-painting room into one. Having supplies like charms and design examples would be great.

Q: Any advice for new students using Slugworks?
A: Don’t be intimidated. The machines look scary at first, but the staff is really friendly, and the training is easy to follow. If you want to try something new, go for it.
