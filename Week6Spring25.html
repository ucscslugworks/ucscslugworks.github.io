This week, I spoke with Quincy Paulos, a first year Plant Sciences major and Slugworks staff, about her creative street sign projects, her love of laser cutters, and how a mysterious Reddit post inspired one of her favorite builds. Quincy blends humor, hands-on building, and curiosity into everything she makes.

Q: How did you get involved in Slugworks?
A: I came here during Slug Day when they were doing tours and thought it was cool. My dad runs a makerspace at UC Berkeley and is connected to a network of makerspaces. He told me I should check Slugworks out. I started spending time here and really liked it.

Q: What machines do you use the most, and which ones are your favorite?
A: I use the laser cutters the most, they’re definitely my favorite. I also use the 3D printers and the sticker printer.

Q: What was the inspiration for your project?
A: I love weird, eclectic graffiti and I’m interested in city planning. I saw an iceberg chart of UCSC urban legends on Reddit and one line just said “Invisible Bears at Science Hill.” I had no idea what it meant, but I thought it’d make a great street sign.

Q: Can you walk me through the process of creating this project?
A: I cut down a piece of stainless steel using the foot shear, then rounded the corners with a felt sander using a wooden template I made. I covered the surface in yellow vinyl, trimmed it, and used removable vinyl as a stencil to spray on the black lettering. Then I peeled it off and drilled mounting holes. That was the basic process.

Q: What challenges did you face while working on this project, and how did you overcome them?
A: Applying the vinyl was tough. It was exactly the same width as the metal, so alignment had to be perfect. I applied it top-down, which led to air bubbles I had to pop one by one. From a distance, though, it looks great.

Q: Did the project change from your original idea?
A: Not really, but I ended up making a second sign, a “Fence Lizard Crossing”, because I’ve been catching a lot of fence lizards around campus and thought it’d be fun to make one for them too.

Q: Do you have any advice for someone who’s interested in getting involved?
A: Don’t let the complicated machines or modules scare you. It’s not as hard as it looks, just start and go through the certifications.

Q: Are there any future projects you’re excited to do?
A: I just started thinking about making a bass guitar. I was talking to Ish (Slugworks Staff) who offered to help, so now I kind of have to. I also want to make a LED street sign that says “Your speed is 35 mph” and then randomly flashes “Help, I’m stuck in the sign.”

Q: Would you like to see anything else down here in the makerspace?
A: I wish we had one of those big paper guillotines. And more woodworking tools like a table saw or jointer planer would be amazing.

Q: How do you think Slugworks contributes to the UCSC community?
A: It’s definitely important. Not everyone has access to tools or space to create, and Slugworks gives students a way to explore and build. I wish the space was bigger, or that we had more maker spaces across campus.

Vote on Measure #85 

Measure 85 supports the future of undergraduate research at UCSC by funding paid opportunities for students to lead and contribute to real-world public impact projects.

The fee sustains a program that is already transforming how students learn through community-focused, project-based work. It continues UCSC’s legacy of experiential learning through efforts like the Everett Program.

It costs just $1.25 per quarter and $0.75 in summer, with 33 percent going to financial aid. All remaining funds directly support student leaders and fellows.

Voting is open May 5 – 16.
