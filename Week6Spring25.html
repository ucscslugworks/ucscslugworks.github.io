This week, I spoke with Scott Phillips, a fourth year Physics major, about his first Slugworks project, a metal flower made using copper, a foot shear, and rivets as a gift for his mom for Mother’s Day.

Q: How did you get involved in Slugworks?
A: I work in a physics lab that requires machining, but the lab doesn’t allow undergraduates to use the shop directly. So I came to Slugworks to get trained and approved to build the enclosures we need.

Q: What machines do you use the most, or what did you use to make your project?
A: For this flower project, I didn’t use any machines. I used a foot shear, tin snips, a screwdriver to score the details, and a pipe for the stem. I’m still waiting for the right project to start using the machines here.

Q: Is this your first personal build project?
A: Yeah, I just got approved to use the shop this week and wanted to start with something I could finish in a day.

Q: What challenges did you face, and how did you overcome them?
A: I originally wanted to solder the petals and leaves on, but the soldering irons in the space weren’t hot enough and were more for PCB work. I ended up using rivets instead, which I had never done before, so that was a fun skill to learn.

Q: What skills have you gained while building this project?
A: I learned how to rivet. It’s simple but really useful, and I had never used one before.

Q: Any future projects you’re excited about?
A: After I posted the flower online, a few people asked to buy one. I might make more and maybe even turn it into a little side project.

Q: Any advice for someone curious about getting involved at Slugworks?
A: Just do the Canvas training. It only took me about two days from starting the process to getting my first project done. It’s really easy to get started if you just go for it.

Q: Anything you’d like to see added to the space?
A: I haven’t been around long enough to know what’s missing. If I had more time, I’d probably join something like Formula Slug, especially to try welding.
