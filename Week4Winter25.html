This week, I spoke with Thomas Liao, a 3rd Year Computer Science Major, about his involvement with Slugworks and the projects he's completed.

Q: How did you first learn about Slugworks?
A: I originally joined the Linux Club, which Julian (Slugworks Staff Member) runs, and we were chatting on Discord. I found out he manages a bunch of 3D printers here, which caught my interest because I needed access to 3D printers for a project. At some point, I wandered into Slugworks and realized how many resources were available. It was kind of a happy accident.

Q: What machines have you used or plan to use for your projects?
A: So far, I’ve only used my Prusa 3D printer at home to work on my bike. But I’d love to use the welding equipment here at Slugworks in the future. Building a bike frame from scratch is on my list of goals, but I need to learn how to weld first. I want to design something that holds batteries properly and looks cleaner.

Q: Tell me more about your bike project. How did it come together?
A: It started as a regular e-bike from a company that later went bankrupt. Right before that happened, I got a replacement wheel under warranty because one of the gears in the motor broke. I replaced the nylon gears with metal ones and realized I could use both wheels, which snowballed into adding a bigger motor, a second battery, a custom 3D-printed enclosure, turn signals, a headlight, and even a horn. I basically turned it into an electric motorcycle. Most of this came together over winter break, although I designed parts during finals week.

Q: What advice would you give to students interested in Slugworks?
A: I didn’t realize until recently that I could access this space. I thought it was only for certain members or clubs. Now that I’m here, I’m excited to start using more resources and learning from others. Don’t be afraid to explore and ask questions.

Q: Are there any future projects you’re excited to do?
A: Besides building a bike frame from scratch, I’d love to work on a server-related project. I know the Linux Club and Slug Security have expressed interest in server rack space, and I’d like to help with that. Starting a student organization around servers is something I’m considering.

Q: Would you like to see anything else down here in the maker space? Any specific machines, programs, or meetups?
A: I’d definitely like to see more server-related resources. As for machines, having easier access to welding equipment would be great, especially for people like me who are working on larger-scale projects.

Lead by Design (CMPM 115) Info Sessions:

Location: BE-55

Thursday, January 30th at 1:30 PM

Have you ever wondered what it’s like to teach your own class? Now’s your chance to become a student professor!

Join CMPM 115: Lead by Design, keep your potential topic in mind! Through this 5-unit course, upper division students with a range of technical skill sets they are passionate about sharing will be guided through the process of developing a 3 unit engineering project-based design lab. You will then be paid to teach the course to lower division students in a subsequent quarter. Lead by Design requires a team-based application, and is tailored to upper division students.

Applications are now open for Spring Quarter! To have priority, submit by March 8. After this date, positions are approved on a rolling basis.

Fill out this form if you're interested in applying!

Hear from students who’ve already been part of this program:

“One of the highlights of teaching the Design-Build Experience Course has been seeing people in their “aha” moments when they light up and figure something out. That is not something that you see in large engineering classes and lecture halls. It’s also been great to experience this new way of teaching with a more experimental topic, because cybersecurity isn’t really taught in schools yet. It’s been a positive experience for both the instructors and the students, and the students feel comfortable engaging and asking questions.”

– Jacobs Taranenko, 3rd year, 2024

“I think that having classes that are focused on practical learning at the start of engineering education is very valuable and not as common as it should be. It gives you a grounding for the theory classes that you take throughout the rest of your undergraduate experience.”

– Eliot Wachtel, 5th year, 2024
