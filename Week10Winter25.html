This week, I spoke with Rohan Person, a Fourth Year Network and Digital Technology major, about their experience organizing NorCal Knockouts 2025, a combat robotics event that brought together builders from across California. As the event's organizer, Rohan managed logistics and navigated the challenges of hosting a large-scale competition. This year, the event partnered with MESA Day, creating an opportunity for middle and high school students to explore STEM fields and see firsthand the creativity and engineering behind combat robotics.

Q: What is NorCal Knockouts and how did this year's event go?
A: NorCal Knockouts is an annual combat robotics event showcasing the engineering work of Slugotics students to the Santa Cruz community and the rest of California. It is open to the public for competitors, but this year we partnered with MESA Day, which made it a private event. The goal was to introduce middle and high school students to STEM fields, not just as career paths but also as hobbies like combat robotics. We had about 30 competitors and 50 people total, since most teams had multiple members working on their bots. The event ran all day with an average audience of around 60 people.

Q: Were the competitors only from Santa Cruz?
A: There were competitors from all over California. A lot of them came from the Bay Area. Some brought personal bots and are part of teams from the TV show BattleBots. One of those teams, Bunny and David from Team Malice, helped us host the event and provided the arena, which we do not have at UCSC. Other competitors included BAM from Team Valkyrie, Aaron Will, and members of Team Seasonable, which has a world champion title.

Q: What was your role in organizing the event?
A: I handled a lot of the paperwork. There were a ton of forms, red tape, and contradictory legal documents from UC legal that had never been clarified. A big part of my job was making sure everyone had the information they needed and coordinating between different groups.

Q: Did partnering with MESA Day help get the event approved?
A: We originally planned to host it on March 1, and our event space was approved. But then that approval got revoked because MESA had already reserved the space. Instead of canceling, they reached out and invited us to collaborate, which ended up working out really well.

Q: What were the biggest challenges?
A: It is always the paperwork. This is the fourth event I have hosted, and every time, the biggest challenge is dealing with forms and approvals. There is always some document that gets overlooked or is missing, which slows everything down.

Q: What has been your biggest accomplishment across the events you have hosted?
A: Seeing how happy everyone is when they get to compete. It is rewarding to create an event where people can bring their robots and battle against really strong opponents. Some fights lasted the full three minutes, and both bots were barely standing by the end. Providing a space where people can truly test their builds is what makes it worth it.

Q: Are you working on any future events?
A: Yes, I am hosting another event called Nexus Knockout on the 22nd in Sunnyvale at the Nexus Maker Space. We will have multiple weight classes, including one pound antweight, one pound plastic antweight, three pound beetleweight, and fifteen pound bot hockey, which is two teams of three playing hockey with robots. All the details can be found on robotcombatevents.com.

Q: How did you first get into combat robotics?
A: I got into it through Slugbotics. I originally planned to join the MATE program for underwater rovers, but when I saw they had a combat robotics team, I thought it sounded interesting. Derek Tran, who is my boss now on Team Robotic Death Company on BattleBots, helped me build my first combat robot. After that, I built a second one and then started competing on my own.

Q: What do you enjoy more, building or hosting events?
A: Both. I love building combat robots, but I also enjoy running events and making sure other people have a space to compete.

Q: Any final thoughts for people interested in combat robotics?
A: If you like design, art, engineering, or even movies like Real Steel, you should check out a combat robotics event. It is a ton of fun, and you will meet great people doing really creative work. Some of the designs are wild. People use things you would never expect, like pineapple skin as a flame retardant to stop their bot from catching fire. If you get the chance, come watch a match. You will not regret it.

CMPM 17 Spring Offerings

Head to the Enrollments tab on My.UCSC.edu and search under Computational Media to enroll in a course below!

CMPM 17-01: Introduction to Hacking Competitions: Introduction to Hacking Competitions is a course centered around introducing students to cybersecurity through a competitive aspect. Students will learn and demonstrate their skills in various cybersecurity categories, with a final project centered around a team based hacking competition. These skills can be transferred to cybersecurity related opportunities and competitions alike and will also boost technical literacy with everyday computer usage. No prior experience is needed. Learners of all experiences are welcome.

CMPM 17-03: Introduction to Creative Design in Virtual Reality: In Creative Design in Virtual Reality (VR) students will learn the principles of 3D application design, with a focus on VR applications. Students will practice their skills by working on creative projects throughout the quarter using industry standard tools such as ShapesXR and Blender and then prototype their own unique app in collaboration with their peers. Students will be able to use this knowledge to kickstart their VR careers and start developing their app portfolio. No prior experience required.
