This week, I spoke with Jay Shepherd, a Fourth Year Film and Digital Media major and Legal Studies minor, about their senior exit film, The Rise and Fall of Starboy. Jay shared how they spent over a year developing this deeply personal project, how Slugworks played a crucial role in building iconic set pieces, and what it was like leading a 20-person crew while navigating the creative chaos of student filmmaking.

Q: What is The Rise and Fall of Starboy about, and where did the idea come from?
A: It is a 15-minute short film about a video store clerk who is drifting through life until he is given a surreal opportunity to become "Starboy," a famous musician. As he steps into that role, he starts to lose a version of himself he did not even know was there. It is about transformation, fame, performance, and identity. It is also a trans allegory. I wanted to explore how trying to conform can cause you to lose yourself, which is something I have experienced firsthand as a transmasc queer person.

Q: How did this evolve into your senior exit project?
A: Most students only get ten weeks to make their senior film, which is not enough time to develop something meaningful. I applied to the Integrated Critical Practice concentration so I could take a full year to write the script, gather my crew, scout locations, and raise money. I had already raised $1,000 by the time class started. I wanted to avoid the last-minute scramble most people go through. That was the plan, at least.

Q: What role did Slugworks play in bringing your film to life?
A: Slugworks honestly saved me. I built a retro talk show set and needed a big custom sign to make it feel real. My original plan was plywood and paint, but that would have taken forever and been way too heavy. I went to Slugworks kind of freaking out, and Ray (Slugworks Staff) suggested using vinyl and cardboard. It was perfect. We printed the logo, mounted it on cardboard, and hung it with fishing wire. It looked amazing on camera. We did the same thing for a star-shaped guitar prop. That one shortcut made the whole set feel professional.

Q: What were some of the biggest challenges you faced, and how did the reality differ from your expectations?
A: Time management, for sure. I made super detailed schedules, even down to ten-minute blocks, and things still ran late. We planned to finish set building by one in the afternoon and ended up finishing at six. That led to sleepless nights, burnout, and trying to stay ahead of everything. I also had to scale back a lot of my original vision. I wanted a massive audience, a limo, big party scenes. I ended up using tighter shots, repurposing locations, and working with about 30 extras instead of 100. Even with all that, I still told the story I wanted to tell.

Q: Do you have a favorite moment from the process?
A: After we finished the talk show set, my set designer pulled out the original 3D mockup and held it up next to the real thing. We just stood there and said, "It is real. We actually made this." That moment really hit me. Also, the night we watched the first rough cut, or more like six in the morning. We were all exhausted, but it looked so good. I almost cried.

Q: What advice would you give to other students working on ambitious creative projects?
A: Use your resources. Go to Slugworks. Apply for grants, even if you think you will not get them. I got rejected a bunch of times but still raised $3,000 through school funding and GoFundMe. Also, be proud of your work. It is okay to say, "I worked hard on this and it turned out great." You have to be your own biggest supporter sometimes.

Q: Do you plan to expand Starboy into something more after graduation?
A: Yes. This short was always meant to be a proof of concept. I want to write a feature-length version and submit it to development programs or pitch it to production companies. The dream is to show what I pulled off with almost no budget and no time, and say, "Imagine what I could do with actual support."

Q: How did working with Slugworks shift your approach as a filmmaker?
A: It made me rethink everything. I tend to overcomplicate things and try to do everything the hardest way possible. Slugworks helped me realize that the simplest solution is often the smartest one. The printer cutter and vinyl idea was one of the best decisions I made.

Q: Why do you think having a space like Slugworks on campus matters?
A: Because anyone can walk in with an idea and walk out with something real. You do not need to be an engineer or an expert. Slugworks gives you access to tools, advice, and support. My film would not look the way it does without it.
