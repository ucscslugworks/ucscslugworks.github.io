This week, I spoke with Tori Lubahn, a 4th Year Electrical Engineering Major about her involvement with Slugworks and the projects she's completed.

Q: How did you get involved in Slugworks?
A: I knew there were club spaces here for robotics and rocketry, but a friend told me about the Canvas course for laser cutter certification. I used to build mechanical wooden models growing up, so I was really excited to learn how to use the tools here. I got certified, did the walkthroughs, and have been using the space ever since!

Q: What machines do you know how to use or work with the most?
A: The laser cutter is my favorite, but I’ve also used sewing machines before.

Q: Which of the projects that you’ve worked on is your favorite?
A: I really enjoyed making custom keychains by tracing portraits and laser cutting them. I also made a wooden box for my mom, where I learned about bending wood and creating intricate designs—it turned out so cute!

Q: What skills have you gained since working at Slugworks?
A: I’ve learned Adobe Illustrator, a little bit of Onshape, and how a laser cutter works in general. It’s more involved than I expected, but it’s been really helpful to learn.

Q: Do you have any advice for someone who’s interested in getting involved?
A: It’s super easy! Just complete the quick Canvas course, and the staff here are really helpful. You don’t need to know anything beforehand—they’ll teach you everything you need.

Q: Are there any future projects you’re excited to do?
A: I’d love to make more keychains since that was so fun. I’m also thinking about creating wall décor and picture frames out of wood.

Q: Would you like to see anything else down here in the maker space? Any specific machines, programs, or meetups?
A: Honestly, I think the space is great as it is. I’m not sure what’s missing, but everything I’ve needed so far has been here.

Q: How do you think your experience at Slugworks has shaped your approach to engineering?
A: It’s given me a much more hands-on approach compared to the lectures I’m used to at UCSC. I really appreciate the opportunity to create and build things in such a practical way.

Q: How do you think Slugworks contributes to the UCSC community? Do you think it’s necessary to have a space like this?
A: I love that you don’t have to be an engineer to use Slugworks. It’s a place where anyone can come and build things, no matter their major. I’ve met people from all kinds of fields: arts, business, etc., and it’s been amazing to see the diverse projects everyone is working on.

Have you ever wondered what it’s like to teach your own class? Now’s your chance to become a student professor!

Join CMPM 115: Lead by Design, keep your potential topic in mind! Through this 5-unit course, upper division students with a range of technical skill sets they are passionate about sharing will be guided through the process of developing a 3 unit engineering project-based design lab. You will then be paid to teach the course to lower division students in a subsequent quarter. Lead by Design requires a team-based application, and is tailored to upper division students.

Applications are now open for Spring Quarter! To have priority, submit by March 8. After this date, positions are approved on a rolling basis.

Fill out this form if you're interested in applying!

Hear from students who’ve already been part of this program:

“One of the highlights of teaching the Design-Build Experience Course has been seeing people in their “aha” moments when they light up and figure something out. That is not something that you see in large engineering classes and lecture halls. It’s also been great to experience this new way of teaching with a more experimental topic, because cybersecurity isn’t really taught in schools yet. It’s been a positive experience for both the instructors and the students, and the students feel comfortable engaging and asking questions.”

– Jacobs Taranenko, 3rd year, 2024
