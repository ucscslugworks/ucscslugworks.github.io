This week, I spoke with Tien Le, a third year Computer Science: Game Design major, about her first project at Slugworks. On just her second visit, she completed the process of heat pressing printed custom designs onto hoodies for the Slug Anime and Manga Association, turning a personal design into wearable merch.

Q: How did you get involved in Slugworks?
A: This is actually only my second time here. I came by for the first time yesterday. I wanted to make something personalized for my club, and this space seemed perfect for it.

Q: What machines or tools did you use to make this project?
A: I drew the graphics in Clip Studio Paint using a raster brush, then brought the design into Illustrator to vectorize it for the print and cut process. Today I finished the project by heat pressing the designs onto the hoodies.

Q: What was the inspiration behind your project?
A: The main inspiration was OMOCAT’s merch. This design is kind of a dupe of one of their sweatshirts, the hololive one. I really liked the style and wanted to make something similar for our club.

Q: Did you run into any challenges while working on it?
A: Yeah, mostly with the trace in Illustrator. Since I used a raster brush, it created funny paths when I scaled it up. Some transparencies and outlines weren’t clean. Also, it was my first time here, so I had to figure out the equipment and how everything works.

Q: What advice would you give to someone who’s interested in getting involved?
A: Just show up and talk to people. Everyone I’ve met so far has been super friendly and really interested in seeing you make something you’re excited about.

Q: Would you like to see anything else down here, like specific machines, programs, or meetups?
A: I’m not super familiar with what you already have, but more outreach would help. I only heard about this space through someone else, and I think that’s true for a lot of people.

Q: Are there any future projects you’re thinking of working on?
A: Probably more stuff for the Anime Club, themed around our mascot. I’m thinking keychains, plushies, anime figures, things like that. Maybe 3D printing or sewing more clothes too.

Vote on Measure #85 

Measure 85 supports the future of undergraduate research at UCSC by funding paid opportunities for students to lead and contribute to real-world public impact projects.

The fee sustains a program that is already transforming how students learn through community-focused, project-based work. It continues UCSC’s legacy of experiential learning through efforts like the Everett Program.

It costs just $1.25 per quarter and $0.75 in summer, with 33 percent going to financial aid. All remaining funds directly support student leaders and fellows.
